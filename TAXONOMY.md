# Taxonomy: Standardized Categorization Schema

This document defines the core taxonomic structures for the Personal Relationship OS, derived from the product constitution and relationship model. These categories provide a consistent vocabulary for understanding human connections while strictly avoiding transactional CRM logic.

## 1. Domains
**Description:** The high-level spheres of life where a relationship primarily operates.
**Allowed Values:** `Personal`, `Professional`, `Community`, `Hybrid`
**When to use:** To establish the baseline context of a relationship's origin or its primary theater of interaction today.
**When not to use:** To artificially restrict a person's identity. If a colleague becomes a close friend, the domain shifts to `Hybrid` or `Personal`; it should not enforce artificial professional boundaries.

## 2. Roles
**Description:** The relational archetype defining the nature of the connection.
**Allowed Values:** `Family`, `Partner`, `Friend`, `Mentor/Guide`, `Colleague/Collaborator`, `Acquaintance`
**When to use:** To define the expected dynamic, historical depth, and natural boundaries inherent in the relationship.
**When not to use:** As a rigid, permanent label. Roles are descriptive, not prescriptive, and must be allowed to naturally evolve.

## 3. Circles
**Description:** The degree of intimacy, trust, and structural tie strength.
**Allowed Values:** `Core` (Strong ties), `Orbit` (Weak ties), `Peripheral` (Extended network)
**When to use:** To guide prioritization of deep reflection versus light nurturing. Defines who requires intensive emotional presence versus gentle awareness.
**When not to use:** As a ranking system or scoreboard. Being in the `Orbit` circle is a healthy, intended state for many valuable relationships, not a failure to reach the `Core`.

## 4. Interaction Channels
**Description:** The medium through which communication or shared experience occurs.
**Allowed Values:** `In-Person`, `Voice/Call`, `Video`, `Asynchronous Text` (Chat/SMS), `Long-Form Written` (Email/Letter)
**When to use:** To log the nature of an interaction and identify patterns (e.g., noticing a relationship has devolved entirely to low-bandwidth text and might benefit from an in-person anchor).
**When not to use:** To mandate communication styles. Use only to reflect reality.

## 5. Interaction Directions
**Description:** The flow of initiation and energy in a specific interaction.
**Allowed Values:** `Initiated` (User reached out), `Received` (Contact reached out), `Mutual` (Planned together), `Serendipitous` (Chance encounter)
**When to use:** To track energetic balance over long time horizons and notice if the user is consistently the sole initiator, prompting reflection on reciprocity and capacity.
**When not to use:** As a short-term tit-for-tat ledger. Healthy relationships experience asymmetrical seasons of initiation.

## 6. Hook Categories
**Description:** Contextual anchors, shared interests, or memory triggers that provide natural, unforced reasons to reconnect.
**Allowed Values:** `Life Event` (Birthdays, moves, career changes), `Shared Interest` (Hobbies, media, art), `Shared Struggle` (Health, grief, challenges), `Aspirational Goal` (Projects, dreams), `Resource/Recommendation` (Books, links, introductions)
**When to use:** To capture salient details during reflections that can be used authentically later to show someone they were deeply heard and remembered.
**When not to use:** As artificial "excuses to touch base." Hooks must be derived from genuine past conversations and care.

## 7. Action Types
**Description:** The functional nature of an interaction or an intended next step in nurturing the connection.
**Allowed Values:** `Check-in` (Light touch), `Deep Conversation` (Vulnerable exchange), `Shared Experience` (Activity, meal, event), `Support/Service` (Helping in a time of need), `Celebration` (Honoring a milestone)
**When to use:** To categorize reflections or set intentional, thoughtful plans for how to show up for someone in the future.
**When not to use:** As a rigid task list or productivity checklist.

## 8. Relationship Lifecycle Stages
**Description:** The current phase of the relationship's dynamic rhythm.
**Allowed Values:** `Incubation` (The Seed), `Cultivation` (The Tending), `Maturation` (The Anchor), `Drift` (The Winter), `Renewal` (The Spring)
**When to use:** To understand the macro-season of a relationship and apply the appropriate level of energy and patience.
**When not to use:** With judgment or panic. Stages like `Drift` are natural, cyclical, and require acceptance, not frantic intervention.

## 9. Value Exchange Categories
**Description:** The type of non-transactional value created, provided, or received between individuals.
**Allowed Values:** `Emotional Support/Sanctuary`, `Intellectual Stimulation/Growth`, `Joy/Play`, `Perspective/Witnessing`, `Practical Assistance`
**When to use:** To reflect on what makes a relationship nourishing and what the user is uniquely positioned to provide to the other person.
**When not to use:** To calculate Return on Investment (ROI) or assess the "net worth" of a human being.

## 10. Contact Sources
**Description:** The historical origin or initial connective tissue that brought the individuals together.
**Allowed Values:** `Organic History` (Childhood, neighborhood, school), `Work/Industry`, `Community/Interest Group`, `Introduction/Mutual Tie`, `Serendipity`
**When to use:** To document the origin story of the relationship, honor its roots, and understand broader network clusters.
**When not to use:** To permanently confine the relationship to its past context. A relationship sourced from a workplace can evolve entirely beyond it.
